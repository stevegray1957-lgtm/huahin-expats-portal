🛂 Visa & Immigration

The most important step before you move. Getting the right visa from the start saves enormous hassle later.

The Thailand Retirement Visa (Non-OA)

For most expats aged 50 and over, the Non-Immigrant O-A visa (commonly called the retirement visa) is the path of choice. It allows you to stay in Thailand for one year at a time, with annual renewals, and is renewable indefinitely as long as you meet the requirements.

Eligibility requirements:

  • Aged 50 or older
  • No criminal record
  • No prohibited communicable diseases (TB, elephantiasis, drug addiction, leprosy, syphilis)
  • Not prohibited from entering Thailand

Financial Requirements

You must meet one of these three financial criteria:

  • Option A: 800,000 THB (approx. £18,000 / $22,000) in a Thai bank account, maintained throughout the year
  • Option B: Monthly income or pension of at least 65,000 THB/month (approx. £1,450 / $1,800)
  • Option C: Combination — bank balance + income totalling 800,000 THB annually
💡 Tip Most expats use Option A (bank deposit). Open a Bangkok Bank account first — they have the best relationship with Thai immigration and issue the required bank letters quickly.

Health Insurance (Mandatory Since 2019)

As of October 2019, health insurance is a legal requirement for the Non-OA visa. Minimum coverage required:

  • Outpatient (OPD): 40,000 THB minimum
  • Inpatient (IPD): 400,000 THB minimum

See our Healthcare section below for recommended insurers, or visit our Expat Essentials page to compare policies.

How to Apply

  1. Apply at the Thai Embassy or Consulate in your home country (recommended for first-time applicants)
  2. Gather required documents: valid passport (18+ months remaining), bank statement/income proof, criminal background check, health certificate, insurance certificate, passport photos
  3. Pay the visa fee (varies by country — approximately £100–£200)
  4. Receive a 90-day Non-OA entry visa, then extend to 1 year at Hua Hin Immigration Office

Once in Hua Hin

Annual extension: Visit Hua Hin Immigration Office each year to extend your stay. Fee: 1,900 THB. You'll need to show your financial documents again.

90-day reporting: You must report your address to immigration every 90 days. This can be done in person at the immigration office, by post, or online via the Thai Immigration website.

TM30: Your landlord is legally required to register your address within 24 hours of your arrival. Make sure they do this — immigration will check.

Re-entry permit: If you plan to leave Thailand and return without losing your visa status, purchase a multiple re-entry permit (3,800 THB) before you leave.

Other Visa Options

  • Long-Term Resident (LTR) Visa — Thailand's premium 10-year visa for wealthy retirees ($80k+ income), remote workers, and skilled professionals. Faster processing, fewer bureaucratic requirements.
  • Non-Immigrant O (Family) — if married to a Thai national
  • Thailand Elite Visa — paid membership (from 900,000 THB / ~£20,000) for 5–20 year stays. Minimal requirements, VIP airport service.
  • Tourist visa / METV — for those still deciding, a 6-month multiple-entry tourist visa allows extended stays while you find your feet

Rent & Accommodation

  • Studio condo: 8,000–15,000 THB/month (good location, pool included)
  • 1-bedroom condo: 12,000–22,000 THB/month
  • 2-bedroom condo/townhouse: 18,000–35,000 THB/month
  • 3-bed pool villa: 40,000–80,000 THB/month
  • Large luxury villa: 80,000–200,000+ THB/month

Negotiate for longer leases — 6 or 12-month contracts typically get 10–20% off monthly rates. Prices vary by area: Khao Takiab and Nong Kae are slightly cheaper; central Hua Hin near the market commands a premium.

Food

  • Street food / local market meal: 60–120 THB
  • Mid-range Thai restaurant: 150–300 THB per person
  • Western/expat restaurant: 300–600 THB per person
  • Weekly supermarket shop (Makro/Big C): 2,000–4,500 THB
  • Beer at a local bar: 80–150 THB
  • Coffee at a cafe: 60–120 THB

Utilities

  • Electricity: 1,500–4,000 THB/month depending on AC usage. Budget for more in hot season (March–May).
  • Water: 200–500 THB/month
  • High-speed fibre internet: 600–900 THB/month (True Move or AIS)
  • Phone SIM with data: 350–700 THB/month for unlimited data packages

Transport

  • Scooter rental: 3,000–4,500 THB/month (or buy a second-hand one for 20,000–40,000 THB)
  • Car rental (long term): 12,000–18,000 THB/month
  • Songthaew (shared local bus): 20–50 THB per trip around town
  • Grab taxi (app): 100–250 THB for most local journeys
  • Bangkok minivan: 200–250 THB per person
💡 Bottom line A couple can live comfortably in Hua Hin — nice condo, eating out regularly, gym membership, golf once a week — for around 90,000–120,000 THB/month (£2,000–£2,700). The 800,000 THB retirement visa deposit requirement represents less than 9 months of comfortable living expenses.

🏡 Finding Accommodation

Hua Hin has a wide range of accommodation — from modern condos to pool villas. Here's how to find the right place.

Renting vs Buying

Renting is by far the most common choice for new expats, and for good reason. Thai property law restricts foreign freehold ownership of land, so most expats either rent, buy a condo (foreigners can own condo units outright in buildings where foreign ownership doesn't exceed 49%), or use Thai company structures for houses.

Unless you're very settled and plan to stay 10+ years, renting gives you flexibility and keeps your capital liquid.

Best Areas in Hua Hin

  • Central Hua Hin (Soi 51–94): Walking distance to the Night Market, beach, restaurants and amenities. Most convenient but slightly more expensive and busier.
  • Khao Takiab (south): More relaxed, quieter beach, slightly cheaper. Good for those who prefer a slower pace.
  • Nong Kae / Soi 112: Very popular with expats. Slightly south of centre, quieter, good mix of condos and houses, access to good supermarkets.
  • Blue Lagoon / Lake areas: Peaceful residential zones, popular for pool villas, 10–15 min drive to centre.
  • Pranburi (30km south): For those wanting a truly quiet, rural environment while remaining close to Hua Hin's amenities.

Tips for Finding a Place

  • Come for a 1-month trial stay before committing to a long lease — short-term rentals are easy to find
  • Use local Facebook groups (search "Hua Hin Rentals" or "Expats Hua Hin") — many landlords advertise here directly with no agent fees
  • Walk the streets in your preferred area — many good rentals are never listed online
  • Use a reputable local real estate agent for higher-end properties or if you want professional help
  • Always get your lease in English and Thai, signed by the landlord
  • Confirm the landlord will complete your TM30 registration within 24 hours of arrival

🏥 Healthcare

Hua Hin has excellent private healthcare. Costs are a fraction of the UK, USA or Australia — but you still need insurance.

Hospitals & Clinics

Bangkok Hospital Hua Hin is the main private hospital and the preferred choice for most expats. It has international-standard facilities, English-speaking doctors, and handles everything from routine checkups to complex procedures. It also directly bills most international insurance providers.

San Paulo Hospital is a good alternative, slightly cheaper for routine care, with English-speaking staff.

Government hospitals (Hua Hin Hospital, Pranburi Hospital) are very cheap but have limited English-language support and longer waiting times — fine for minor issues if you're budget-conscious.

There are also many excellent dental clinics in Hua Hin offering high-quality treatment at a fraction of home-country prices. Dental work is one of the genuine bargains of expat life in Thailand.

Typical Costs (Private)

  • GP consultation: 500–1,500 THB
  • Specialist consultation: 1,000–3,000 THB
  • Dental cleaning: 500–1,200 THB
  • Blood test panel: 1,500–4,000 THB
  • X-ray: 500–1,500 THB
  • Appendectomy (example surgery): 80,000–200,000 THB

Health Insurance

Health insurance is mandatory for the Non-OA retirement visa (minimum 40,000 THB OPD / 400,000 THB IPD). Even outside of visa requirements, it's strongly recommended — a serious illness or accident without cover can be financially devastating.

Two main options:

  • International health insurance (e.g. AXA Global Healthcare, Cigna, Allianz): covers you worldwide, higher premiums but maximum flexibility. Best for those who travel frequently or split time between countries.
  • Local Thai health insurance: covers treatment within Thailand only, significantly cheaper. Fine if you'll be based here full-time.

Costs: Expect to pay 3,000–6,000 THB/month for a decent local plan at age 55–65, or 8,000–15,000 THB/month for comprehensive international cover.

⚠️ Important Pre-existing conditions may be excluded or come with a loading on premiums. Always declare everything honestly when applying — claims denied for non-disclosure are a common problem.

🏦 Banking & Money

Getting your finances set up correctly is one of the most important early tasks. Do this right and save hundreds every year.

Opening a Thai Bank Account

You'll need a Thai bank account to satisfy the immigration financial requirements for a retirement visa. Bangkok Bank is the most popular choice for expats — they have good relationships with foreign banks, English-language banking, and issue the required bank confirmation letters for immigration quickly.

What you'll need to open an account:

  • Passport (original)
  • Non-Immigrant visa (tourist visas work at some branches but Bangkok Bank typically requires a Non-O or Non-OA)
  • Proof of address in Thailand (utility bill, lease agreement)
  • Initial deposit (typically 500–1,000 THB)

Other good options: Kasikorn Bank (KBank) has excellent mobile banking app; SCB (Siam Commercial Bank) is also popular. Avoid opening multiple accounts — immigration only needs to see one.

Transferring Money from Abroad

This is where most expats throw away money unnecessarily. Bank wire transfers typically cost 3–5% in fees and poor exchange rates. On a £2,000 monthly pension, that's £60–£100 lost every single month.

Wise (formerly TransferWise) uses the real mid-market exchange rate and charges only a small transparent fee — typically 0.4–0.6% on GBP to THB transfers. On £2,000, that's around £8–12 versus £60–100 for a bank transfer. The savings add up to hundreds of pounds per year.

You can transfer directly from Wise into your Bangkok Bank account. Set up your account before you leave home — it's easier to verify your identity in your home country.

ATMs in Hua Hin

ATMs are plentiful throughout Hua Hin. However, Thai ATMs charge a 220 THB foreign card fee per withdrawal. If you're withdrawing regularly, this adds up fast. Set up a Wise account or a fee-free card like Revolut or Charles Schwab (US) to minimise ATM fees.

Maximum ATM withdrawal is typically 20,000–30,000 THB per transaction. Use Bangkok Bank ATMs first — they're most reliable for foreign cards.

Tax Considerations

Tax obligations vary depending on your nationality and how long you spend in Thailand. As of 2024, Thailand changed its rules: foreign income remitted to Thailand in the same tax year it was earned is now taxable. Consult a tax professional familiar with both your home country and Thai tax law.

💡 Tip The UK and Thailand have a double-taxation agreement. UK state pension income is not taxable in Thailand. Private pension income may be taxable — get specific advice for your situation.

✈️ Getting Here

Hua Hin is easy to reach from Bangkok's airports. Here's your guide to getting there — and getting around once you arrive.

Flights to Thailand

There is no major international airport in Hua Hin itself (Hua Hin Airport handles limited domestic and charter flights). Most international expats fly into one of Bangkok's two airports:

  • Suvarnabhumi (BKK) — Main international airport, 200km from Hua Hin (~2.5–3 hours by road)
  • Don Mueang (DMK) — Budget airlines hub, 220km from Hua Hin (~2.5–3 hours by road)

Direct flights from the UK take around 11–12 hours. From Australia, expect 9–10 hours (via KL or Singapore). From the US, 17–20 hours with one stop.

Bangkok Airport to Hua Hin

  • Private transfer (recommended): 2,000–2,500 THB. Book in advance through your hotel or a transfer company. Comfortable, door-to-door, meets you at arrivals.
  • Lomprayah / Lompraya minivan: Around 250 THB/person from Victory Monument or direct airport pickup services. Takes 3–4 hours with stops.
  • Train: There's a Hua Hin train station (the beautiful old colonial-style station). Trains from Bangkok's Hua Lamphong take 3–5 hours. Cheap and scenic but slow.
  • Bus: Southern Bus Terminal (Sai Tai Mai) in Bangkok to Hua Hin bus station. Around 150–200 THB, 3.5–4 hours.

Getting Around Hua Hin

  • Scooter — The expat favourite. Easy to rent, park anywhere, cheap to run. International driving licence required (technically). Respect Thai traffic — it can be chaotic.
  • Car — More practical for families or those who prefer air-conditioned comfort. Long-term car rental from 12,000 THB/month.
  • Songthaew (shared pickup truck taxis) — Run fixed routes around town for 20–50 THB. Flag them down on major roads.
  • Grab (Uber equivalent) — Works well in Hua Hin. Convenient, fair prices, cashless payment option.
  • Bicycle — Hua Hin is reasonably flat and has some cycle paths. Great for exploring the area at a leisurely pace.
