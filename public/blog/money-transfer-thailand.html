Best Ways to Transfer Money to Thailand as an Expat (2026)

Updated: April 2026 Reading time: 9 min Category: Money & Banking

Getting money from your home country into your Thai bank account is something almost every expat in Hua Hin has to deal with regularly. Whether you receive a pension from the UK, rental income from a property back in Australia, a salary paid in US dollars, or investment withdrawals from a Canadian account, you need a reliable, cost-effective way to convert and move those funds to Thailand. The difference between doing this well and doing it badly can easily amount to hundreds — or even thousands — of dollars per year. This guide explains your real options, their actual costs, and the approach most Hua Hin expats settle on once they have been here long enough to compare the alternatives properly.

Why This Matters More Than You Might Think

The foreign exchange market is one of the most lucrative businesses in the world precisely because most consumers never scrutinise the rate they are being given. When your bank in the UK or US advertises "international transfers," they typically show you a headline exchange rate that looks reasonable. What they do not prominently advertise is that the rate they use is not the real mid-market rate — the one you can look up on Google or XE.com — but a marked-up version of it. That markup, typically between 2.5% and 5% depending on the bank and the currency pair, is the bank's profit on your transaction. Add a flat transfer fee of anywhere from £10 to £35 per transaction, and the costs compound quickly.

On a transfer of £1,000, a 4% exchange rate markup costs you £40 before the wire fee is even applied. Transfer £1,500 per month to cover living expenses and that is around £600 per year lost purely to poor exchange rates and fees — money that could be funding a few extra dinners at Hua Hin's excellent seafood restaurants, covering your health insurance premium, or simply staying in your pocket.

The Problem with Traditional Bank Wire Transfers

The default option for most new expats is to instruct their home bank to send an international wire transfer (SWIFT transfer) to their Thai bank account. This works, in the sense that money does eventually arrive. But the process has several significant drawbacks:

  • Poor exchange rates: As noted above, banks apply a spread of 2.5–5% above the mid-market rate. On large transfers this is a serious cost.
  • Multiple fees: Your sending bank charges a fee, the receiving bank (Bangkok Bank, Kasikorn, SCB) typically charges a receiving fee of 200–500 THB, and there may be a correspondent bank fee in the middle of the chain.
  • Slow delivery: SWIFT transfers between the UK/US/Australia and Thailand typically take 2–5 business days. In an emergency that is a long time to wait.
  • Opaque process: Tracking a SWIFT transfer is notoriously difficult. If something goes wrong — the account number is slightly off, the receiving bank rejects the transfer — it can take weeks to resolve and get your money returned.
  • High minimum costs: Even on small transfers, the flat fee applies. Sending £200 and paying £25 in fees means you have already lost 12.5% before the exchange rate markup.

For expats who transferred money home infrequently — perhaps once a year for a big purchase — the bank's convenience factor may have made these costs acceptable. When you are transferring every month, they absolutely are not.

Wise (Formerly TransferWise): The Best Option for Most Expats

Wise has fundamentally changed the economics of international money transfer since it launched, and it is now the go-to service for the majority of experienced expats in Hua Hin. The core principle is simple: Wise uses the real mid-market exchange rate — the same rate shown on Google or Reuters — with no markup whatsoever. Their profit comes from a small, transparent, flat-percentage fee shown clearly before you confirm the transfer. On a GBP to THB transfer in 2026, that fee is typically around 0.4–0.6% of the transfer amount.

Compare that to a bank charging 3.5% markup plus a £25 wire fee on a £1,000 transfer, and the maths is stark: the bank costs you roughly £60; Wise costs you roughly £5–6. On a monthly £1,500 transfer, that difference is approximately £650 per year.

How Wise Works

You create an account at wise.com (free), verify your identity with a passport scan and selfie, and link your home country bank account or debit card as a funding source. To send money to Thailand, you enter the amount you want to send, the destination currency (THB), and your Thai bank account details. Wise shows you exactly how much THB will arrive in your account before you confirm. Payment typically takes 1–2 business days and arrives directly in your Bangkok Bank, Kasikorn, or SCB account.

Wise also offers a multi-currency account with a debit card, which lets you hold balances in GBP, USD, EUR, AUD, and dozens of other currencies and convert between them at will. This is particularly useful if your income arrives in multiple currencies or if you want to wait for a favourable rate before converting to THB.

Setting Up a Wise Account Before You Leave Home

The verification process for Wise is straightforward but takes a day or two for new accounts. It is strongly advisable to set up and verify your Wise account while you are still in your home country. This makes the identity verification process smoother (you can use your home address), ensures your home bank account is already linked as a funding source, and means you can send your first transfer the moment you arrive in Thailand and open your local bank account. Attempting to set up Wise for the first time after moving to Thailand occasionally causes complications with address verification.

Other Transfer Options: Honest Assessment

Traditional Bank Wire Transfer

As covered above, this is the most expensive regular option for most currency pairs. It remains useful in specific circumstances: very large transfers where your bank may offer preferential rates for high-value transactions (typically above £50,000), or when the receiving side has a specific requirement for a SWIFT transfer (some Thai government agencies or property conveyancers). For monthly living expenses, it should not be your default.

ATM Withdrawals in Thailand

Using your home country debit card to withdraw cash from Thai ATMs is genuinely convenient and works well as a supplementary option. The exchange rate applied by Visa and Mastercard for card transactions is typically close to the mid-market rate, which is fair. The problem is the fees: every Thai ATM currently charges a flat 220 THB (approximately £5–5.50) per withdrawal to foreign cards, regardless of the amount withdrawn. This fee is applied by the Thai bank operating the ATM and is unavoidable — it applies to every foreign Visa and Mastercard, including those from Wise, Revolut, and Charles Schwab.

If you withdraw 10,000 THB at a time and pay 220 THB in ATM fees, you are losing 2.2% per transaction before your home bank's own foreign transaction fee (if any) is applied. Withdraw smaller amounts more frequently and the percentage lost rises quickly. ATMs are a good option for cash in hand when you need it, but not an efficient way to fund your ongoing living expenses in Thailand.

Tip: To minimise ATM fees, always withdraw the maximum amount the machine allows per transaction (typically 20,000–30,000 THB), and withdraw as infrequently as your cash needs allow. A Wise debit card charges no foreign transaction fees beyond the unavoidable Thai ATM fee.

Cryptocurrency

Some expats investigate using Bitcoin or stablecoins to move money to Thailand, attracted by the idea of bypassing banks entirely. In practice, this is not recommended for most expats. Crypto-to-THB conversion requires using a Thai crypto exchange, which involves additional verification steps, conversion fees, and a further step of moving the THB to your bank account. The process involves more steps, more potential for error, and more tax complexity than simply using Wise. For the typical expat receiving a monthly pension or salary, the added complexity far outweighs any theoretical savings. Wise is simply better.

Remittance Services (Western Union, MoneyGram)

Western Union and MoneyGram operate throughout Thailand and are reliable for cash pickups. Their exchange rates are generally worse than Wise, though better than most banks. They are useful if the recipient in Thailand does not have a bank account or if you need same-day cash delivery. For regular transfers to your own Thai bank account, Wise is the better choice on both cost and convenience.

Which Thai Bank Is Best for Receiving International Transfers?

If you have not yet opened a Thai bank account, or are considering switching, Bangkok Bank deserves mention as the preferred choice for expats who receive regular international transfers. Bangkok Bank has the most established international correspondent banking relationships of any Thai bank, which means SWIFT transfers from UK, US, Australian, and European banks route more cleanly to Bangkok Bank than to some of its competitors. Receiving fees at Bangkok Bank are standard (around 200–500 THB per inbound transfer), but the reduced likelihood of routing errors or delays makes it the safer choice.

For Wise transfers specifically, the destination bank matters less — Wise routes via local payment rails rather than SWIFT, which makes transfers faster and cheaper regardless of which Thai bank you use. Kasikorn Bank (KBank) and SCB are both perfectly good options if you already have accounts there.

Practical Tips for Managing Money Transfers in Hua Hin

  • Set up Wise before you leave your home country. Verification is faster and smoother with a home address.
  • Transfer on weekdays. Currency markets are closed on weekends and the rates applied to weekend transfers are often slightly less favourable. Wise and most services settle transfers initiated on Thursday or Friday on Monday, which can mean a minor rate disadvantage if markets move over the weekend.
  • Consider transferring larger, less frequent amounts rather than small weekly transfers. Even Wise's low fees add up if you transfer every week. A monthly transfer is more efficient.
  • Keep a float in Thailand. Aim to always have 1–2 months of living expenses in your Thai account so you are never forced to transfer at a bad rate due to urgency.
  • Link to Bangkok Bank for SWIFT transfers. If you ever do need to use a bank wire (for a property purchase, for example), Bangkok Bank's international banking relationships make the process smoother.
  • Understand the Thai remittance documentation requirement. Any amount over $50,000 USD (or equivalent) transferred into Thailand in a single transaction may require a Foreign Exchange Transaction Form (FET) from your receiving bank. For smaller regular transfers, this is not an issue.

The Bottom Line

For the overwhelming majority of English-speaking expats in Hua Hin — whether retired on a pension, working remotely, or drawing down investments — Wise is the best tool for regular money transfers to Thailand. The savings compared to bank wire transfers are substantial and compound significantly over a year or more of expat life. Set up your Wise account before you leave home, link it to Bangkok Bank once you arrive, and transfer monthly on a weekday. That simple approach will save most expats several hundred pounds, dollars, or Australian dollars every year compared to using their home bank's international transfer service.
