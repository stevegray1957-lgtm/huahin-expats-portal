Thailand Retirement Visa (Non-OA): Complete Guide for Hua Hin Expats (2026)

Published: April 2026 By the HuaHin ExpatsPortal Team 14 min read

For most English-speaking retirees settling in Hua Hin, the Non-Immigrant O-A visa — universally known as the Non-OA or retirement visa — is the primary long-term legal pathway to living in Thailand. It is well-established, renewable annually, and manageable without legal expertise once you understand the process. That said, the paperwork requirements and financial rules have tightened considerably over the years, and getting things wrong can mean delays, rejection, or worse, an unintended overstay.

This guide walks you through everything: what the visa is, whether you qualify, exactly what documents you need, where to apply from Hua Hin, how to maintain your status once you have it, and the common mistakes to avoid.

What Is the Non-OA Visa?

The Non-Immigrant O-A visa is a long-stay visa issued by the Thai government specifically for foreign nationals aged 50 and over who wish to retire in Thailand. It is not a permanent residence visa — Thailand does not offer an easily accessible permanent residency pathway for most retirees — but it is renewable every year and, in practice, gives you a stable legal basis for living in the country indefinitely as long as you meet the ongoing requirements.

The visa is classified as "Non-Immigrant" because it does not permit employment. You cannot work in Thailand on a Non-OA, not even part-time or freelance work for Thai clients. Remote work for foreign employers sits in a legal grey area that is partly addressed by the newer Long-Term Resident visa (covered at the end of this article).

When first issued from a Thai embassy abroad, the Non-OA gives you a one-year stay (not just a one-year visa — the stamp in your passport allows a full year of residency from the date of entry). After that, you extend it annually at a Thai immigration office — and in Hua Hin, that means the local immigration office near the Bluport/Market Village area of town.

Eligibility Requirements

To qualify for a Non-OA retirement visa, you must meet all of the following:

  • Age: You must be 50 years of age or older at the time of application.
  • Nationality: You must hold a passport from a country not on Thailand's list of prohibited nationalities. This is not an issue for UK, US, Australian, Canadian, EU, or most other Western passport holders.
  • No criminal record: A criminal record check from your home country (or country of current residence) is required, showing no serious criminal convictions. Minor spent offences do not automatically disqualify you, but serious convictions — particularly drug-related or violent offences — will.
  • No prohibited diseases: A health certificate from a licensed physician is required confirming you do not have leprosy, tuberculosis (active), elephantiasis, stage 3 syphilis, or drug addiction. This is a standard medical declaration and rarely causes issues.
  • Valid passport: Your passport must have at least 18 months of remaining validity at the time of application.
  • Financial requirements: You must meet one of three financial thresholds (detailed below).
  • Health insurance: Mandatory since 2019 (detailed below).

Financial Requirements: The 800k Rule Explained

This is the section that causes the most confusion and anxiety for prospective retirees. Thailand requires proof that you can financially support yourself without working. There are three ways to demonstrate this:

Option 1: Money in a Thai Bank Account

Maintain a minimum of 800,000 THB in a Thai bank account (typically a Thai savings account at a bank like Bangkok Bank, Kasikorn, or SCB). The funds must be deposited at least three months before your initial application, and the balance must remain at or above 800,000 THB throughout your stay. Immigration will issue a bank letter confirming this.

Option 2: Monthly Income

Prove a regular monthly income of at least 65,000 THB per month (approximately $1,850 USD at current rates). This is typically demonstrated via an income letter from your embassy. The British, Australian, American, and most European embassies in Bangkok offer this service. Note that some embassies have phased out income letters — the US Embassy, for example, no longer issues them, meaning American retirees generally need to use the bank deposit method or the combination method below.

Option 3: Combination Method

A combination of monthly income and bank deposit that together satisfy the threshold. For example, if you have a regular monthly income of 40,000 THB, you would need a further 200,000 THB in a Thai bank account (with the combined figure meeting the overall requirement). The exact calculation is assessed case by case — consult with a visa agent or directly with immigration before relying on this method.

Important: The 800,000 THB deposit method requires the funds to be in a Thai bank account — not a foreign account. Setting up a Thai bank account requires being in Thailand on a valid visa. Most expats arrive on a tourist visa or visa exemption, open the account, make the deposit, and then apply for the Non-OA extension. The money must stay above 800,000 THB throughout the year and must not drop below 400,000 THB at any point (immigration performs spot checks). Withdrawing below 800,000 THB temporarily and topping up just before renewal is technically a violation and can result in rejection.

Health Insurance: Mandatory Since 2019

Thailand made health insurance compulsory for Non-OA visa holders in October 2019. This requirement applies at both initial application and every annual renewal. The minimum coverage required is:

  • Outpatient (OPD) coverage: at least 40,000 THB per year
  • Inpatient (IPD) coverage: at least 400,000 THB per year

Your insurance must be from a company approved by the Thai Office of Insurance Commission (OIC). Many international insurers operating in Thailand meet this requirement, but always confirm the policy is OIC-approved before purchasing, as immigration will check. The insurer must provide a certificate in a format acceptable to Thai immigration. See our healthcare section for guidance on suitable health insurance options for Hua Hin expats.

Budget-conscious retirees should note that a policy meeting the bare minimum requirements can be found from around 5,000–10,000 THB per year for younger retirees (50s), rising significantly with age. More comprehensive coverage — which is advisable given the potential costs of serious illness — typically runs 30,000–80,000+ THB per year depending on age and pre-existing conditions.

Where and How to Apply

Applying from Outside Thailand (Recommended for First-Time Applicants)

The cleanest way to obtain your first Non-OA is to apply at a Royal Thai Embassy or Consulate in your home country before you move. This gives you a full one-year entry stamp from the moment you arrive in Thailand and avoids the complexity of converting status inside the country.

The application process at a Thai embassy generally involves:

  1. Completing the Thai visa application form (available on the embassy's website or in person)
  2. Submitting the required documents (see below)
  3. Paying the visa fee (typically around 2,000–2,500 THB equivalent in local currency)
  4. Waiting for processing — usually 5–10 working days at most embassies

Call or check the specific embassy's website before visiting, as document requirements and appointment booking procedures vary by country and change periodically.

Applying or Extending Inside Thailand

If you are already in Thailand on a tourist visa or visa exemption entry, you can apply to convert to a Non-OA or extend an existing Non-OA at your local Immigration Office. For Hua Hin residents, this is the Hua Hin Immigration Office, located in the Bluport / Market Village area of town on Phetkasem Road (Hwy 4). It is signposted and well-known to locals. The office is open Monday to Friday, 8:30am to 4:30pm, closed on Thai public holidays.

Arrive early — the office gets busy from mid-morning. Bring all original documents plus copies. Queue number systems vary; some days are fast, others can take a full morning.

Documents Required

Prepare the following for both initial applications and annual renewals (requirements can vary slightly — always confirm the current list with the relevant immigration office or Thai embassy before attending):

  • Passport: Original plus copies of the photo page, current visa/entry stamp page, and all pages with prior Thai visa stamps. Passport must have at least 18 months validity remaining.
  • TM.7 application form: Available at the immigration office or for download from the Thai Immigration Bureau website. Complete in full, in block capitals.
  • Passport-size photographs: Typically 2–4 photos, 4x6cm, with a white or light background. Taken recently (within 6 months). Have several spares — requirements vary.
  • Proof of finances: Either a bank letter from a Thai bank (dated within 7 days of your application) confirming your balance, or an income letter from your embassy. For the Thai bank route, you will also need a copy of your bank book with all pages showing recent transactions.
  • Health insurance certificate: From an OIC-approved insurer, showing OPD/IPD coverage meeting the minimum requirements, valid for the full period of the visa/extension being applied for.
  • Health certificate: Completed and signed by a licensed physician, confirming absence of the prohibited diseases. Issued within the past 3 months. Thai hospitals and clinics can provide this for around 300–600 THB.
  • Criminal record check: From your home country or country of most recent long-term residence. Issued within 3 months of application. In the UK this is a DBS check; in Australia it is a National Police Check; in the US it is an FBI background check. These take varying amounts of time to obtain — plan ahead.
  • Proof of address: A copy of your rental contract, or a TM.30 receipt (see below) confirming your registered address in Thailand.

Annual Extension

Each year, before your current permission to stay expires, you must visit Hua Hin Immigration Office to extend for another 12 months. The extension fee is 1,900 THB, payable in cash at the office.

You must again demonstrate your financial position (bank letter or income evidence) and provide a current, valid health insurance certificate. The other documents from the initial application (criminal check, health certificate) are generally not required again for extensions — though it is worth double-checking the current requirements, as these can change.

Extensions are typically processed on the same day if you arrive with complete documentation. You will receive a new stamp in your passport granting a further 12 months of stay.

90-Day Reporting

While living in Thailand on a Non-OA, you are legally required to report your address to immigration every 90 days. This is entirely separate from the annual extension — it is an address confirmation, not a visa renewal. The options are:

  • In person: Visit Hua Hin Immigration Office with your passport, a completed TM.47 form, and a copy of your passport's photo and stamp pages. No fee.
  • By post: Send a TM.47 form with a self-addressed envelope and document copies to the immigration office. Must arrive before the 90-day deadline.
  • Online: Via the Thai Immigration Bureau's online 90-day reporting system (imm.immigration.go.th). When the system works, it is the most convenient option — but the website is notoriously unreliable. Do not leave it to the last day.

Missing a 90-day report results in a 2,000 THB fine. It does not invalidate your visa, but fines accumulate and a pattern of non-compliance can create problems at renewal.

Multiple Re-Entry Permits

A critical point that catches many new arrivals off guard: your Non-OA allows you to stay in Thailand, but if you leave the country without a re-entry permit, your permission to stay is cancelled and you will need to start the extension process again upon return.

To leave and return without losing your status, you need a re-entry permit, obtained from immigration before you depart:

  • Single re-entry permit: 1,000 THB — allows one trip abroad and back.
  • Multiple re-entry permit: 3,800 THB — allows unlimited trips in and out for the remaining duration of your current permission to stay. Strongly recommended if you travel regularly.

Re-entry permits can be obtained at Hua Hin Immigration Office or at the airport departure hall before you fly. The airport option is convenient but requires arriving early enough to queue. Obtaining it in advance at the immigration office is more reliable.

TM30: Landlord Registration

Under Thai law, any person providing accommodation to a foreign national must report that person's presence to immigration within 24 hours of arrival. This is the TM.30 form, and the obligation rests with the landlord or property owner — not with you as the tenant.

In practice, many landlords in Hua Hin — particularly those experienced with foreign tenants — handle this routinely and will give you a TM.30 receipt automatically. However, in some cases, especially with private landlords or short-term rentals, it gets missed. This matters because: (1) immigration may ask for your TM.30 receipt at your 90-day report or annual extension, and (2) repeated failures to have it can complicate your renewal.

Make TM.30 part of your initial conversation with any landlord. If they are unfamiliar with it, the process is straightforward — they register online via the Thai Immigration Bureau portal or in person at the immigration office.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Letting the bank balance drop below 800,000 THB. Immigration performs random checks and reviews transactions at renewal. Dipping below even once can cause problems. Treat this balance as untouchable.
  • Buying insurance that is not OIC-approved. International policies that are perfectly reputable elsewhere may not satisfy Thai immigration's specific requirement. Always confirm OIC approval with the insurer in writing before purchasing.
  • Leaving Thailand without a re-entry permit. One of the most common and avoidable mistakes. Even a day trip to Malaysia or a flight connection through another country will cancel your stay permission if you have no permit.
  • Missing the 90-day report deadline. Set a calendar reminder 85 days after each report. Do not rely on the online system working on deadline day — go in person or submit by post a few days early.
  • Applying for renewal too early or too late. You can apply for your annual extension up to 30 days before your current stay expires. Do not apply earlier — it will be refused. Do not leave it to the last day — if there is a queue or a document problem, you may overstay.
  • Outdated documents. Bank letters must typically be dated within 7 days of your application. Health certificates within 3 months. Criminal record checks within 3 months for initial applications. Plan the timing carefully so documents do not expire before your appointment.

Alternative Visa Options Worth Knowing About

The Non-OA is the right choice for most retirees, but it is worth briefly understanding the alternatives:

Non-Immigrant O (Non-OB — Business Visa)

For those who plan to work, run a business, or invest in Thailand. Requires a work permit in addition to the visa. Not relevant for retirees with no commercial activity in Thailand.

Long-Term Resident Visa (LTR)

Introduced in late 2022, the LTR visa is Thailand's premium long-stay option aimed at wealthy retirees, remote workers, and skilled professionals. The Wealthy Pensioner category requires proof of passive income of at least $80,000 USD per year (or $40,000/year with assets of $250,000 USD) and a health insurance policy with at least $50,000 USD coverage. In return, you get a 10-year renewable visa, a 20% flat tax rate on assessable income (relevant if you remit foreign income), no 90-day reporting requirement, and a fast-track service at immigration. If you meet the financial threshold, it is worth considering — it dramatically reduces the administrative burden of the Non-OA's annual renewal and reporting cycle.

Multiple-Entry Tourist Visa (METV)

A short-term option allowing multiple 60-day stays (extendable once per entry by 30 days) within a 6-month period. Used by some expats who are not yet ready to commit to a retirement visa or who split time between Thailand and another country. Not a sustainable long-term solution due to the limited total stay allowed and increasingly strict enforcement at borders for those seen to be living in Thailand on tourist entries.

Thailand Elite Visa

A paid membership programme offering 5, 10, or 20-year stays with significant concierge benefits. Costs range from around 600,000 to 2,000,000 THB depending on the tier. No financial proof or health insurance requirements. Appealing for those who can afford the upfront cost and want maximum convenience with minimal annual bureaucracy.
